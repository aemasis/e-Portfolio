Reflection

As my time at Dominican comes to an end, I've spent a lot of time thinking about where I started and where I've ended up. As I was preparing my e-portfolio I was a little afraid I wasn't going to have enough work to show. It turns out that looking through the Canvas pages of past (and current) courses I could, in fact, illustrate all five learning goals and their outcomes with my work. I could also use the skills I'd learnt to build a fully functional website to showcase these artifacts.

When thinking through what artifacts I wanted to share, I thought about my personal journey, professional identity, and the skillsets I've developed while at Dominican. I wanted to choose artifacts that I had spent a lot of time working on and thinking over. One of the main criteria was that the artifact had to represent a broad scope of interests and ability to work across disciplines. Interdisciplinary thinking is a skillset and trait that is very important for me in developing my practice and identity both in the library and information profession and as an independent researcher. I think the more kinds of knowledge we have access to the more tools we have in our professional toolbox. I ended up selecting four essays, three webpages, two proposals, a mis-en-page project, one presentation, and two write ups about my work experiences.

The essays I selected were written for my LIS 775 course, Introduction to Archival Principles, my LIS 707 course, Leadership, Marketing, and Strategic Communication, and my LIS 701 course, Core Values, Ethics, and Issues in The Library and Information Science Profession. The essays from LIS 707 and LIS 775 are two pieces of my portfolio that I consider to be part of my best work.

My Introduction to Archival Principles essay was centered around the concept of "affective archiving" and how archivists and institutions can use ideas from the field of affect studies or affect theory to work with the communities whose materials they are stewarding. The paper focuses on the ways in which archival work tries to balance the tension between being a reflective liberatory space and a space of potential trauma. It looks at how affect theory can be useful in assessing archival practice, anticipating sites of trauma, accepting different forms of knowledge and creating spaces for communal authority those involved in the archival network. I believe this work reflects an understanding of goals one, two, and four. It helped me to work through developing ideas that inform my identity as an archivist and library profession. It was through reading scholarly articles, case studies, and research that I was able to evaluate the potential for affect theory in the archive and understand how to utilize it in situations that call for it.

This essay stemmed from readings I was assigned in LIS 775. These articles were some of my favorites from my time at Dominican. The case studies and research presented in them were thought provoking, insightful, and helped me to make connections between theories of the archive and different theoretical frameworks I have always had an interest in exploring. Critical theory and post-colonial theory were all at play with issues concerning the archive. It was through engaging with academic scholarship on the archive that I was able to further develop my interdisciplinary approach.

The second essay was for my Leadership, Marketing, and Strategic Communication course. This paper borrows concepts from the field of Urban Studies, which was my undergraduate major. It looks at the concept of placemaking and how it can be applied to managing public libraries. Placemaking is a concept that focuses on how people produce the spaces they inhabit through patterns of use and the cultural elements they bring in. The paper shows how placemaking happens in public libraries and what library workers can learn from this. I chose this paper because it takes elements from different areas of interest and creates an interdisciplinary approach to understanding the library. Like the paper for Archival Principles, it demonstrates goals one, two, and four, but in different ways.

I think these two papers, written during the second semester of my first year and first semester of my second year, demonstrate a desire to engage with academic research. The other two essays featured in my e-portfolio are from my first semester at Dominican and while I stand by them, I think there is a clear sense of development in my ability to write an academic paper. I think the inclusion of all four papers demonstrates part of my intellectual and professional growth while in the program. I believe the other artifacts included in my e-portfolio do the same, especially the webpages I included.

The webpages I decided to feature are my other favorite artifacts. Each webpage was designed for a different course, with a different function, topic, and theme. But all three of these websites allowed me to create a space where I could share information, design something, and engage with a topic I have personal interest in.

The first webpage I developed was for my LIS 702 course, Facilitating User Learning. The site acts as an interactive subject guide for anyone interested in learning the fundamentals of textile history. The books selected for the guide each deal with the economic, social, cultural, and political aspects of textiles to ensure that anyone who uses the guide is able to glean a semi-comprehensive picture of the role textiles have played throughout history. Each book has a carefully written abstract to help users select the right books to start with and to guide their research. The guide also includes webpages with robust digital collections of textiles and textile related materials.

The second webpage was the final for my LIS 711 course, Early Books and Manuscripts. This site functioned as a digital exhibition space for manuscripts relating to the history of astronomy. While this website has a page dedicated to citations, its main function is to directly share information on the objects presented. It's more of a curated exhibition of objects meant to tell a story about both the history of astronomy and the history of the book.

The last webpage was created as the final project for INF 253, Intro to Web Design. It was in this course that I learned how to build a website from scratch. The other two web projects strongly depended on prefabricated elements and websites that assisted in the development of the site. My first website, the subject guide, was a one-page website with simple links and images built using Google Pages. The second site was built with Canva, utilizing ready-made elements and Canva's page linking system. It was a step up from the Google Site with its multi-page network and galleries, but I hadn't fully designed it and managing the linking system was more tedious and complex because of this. Intro to Web Design gave me the skill set to build a basic multi-page website from scratch and design it to my specifications and liking.

For my final project I built a website about knitting. It was meant to be an introductory site that gave users the ability to learn about the skills and terminology needed to begin knitting, as well as its historical and cultural significance. The website was designed to be simple to use and to provide information on where to go to advance in the craft. It blended together the historical and instructional elements of my LIS 711 website and the bibliographical purpose of my LIS 702 webpage. These websites helped me to meet MLIS student learning goals three and four.

The other artifacts I would like to highlight are my write-ups of my time at both the Chicago Public Library and the Newberry Library. While these are not artifacts from a specific course, they are related to my time at Dominican and are spaces where I have had the opportunity to really apply my coursework to real life situations. I felt it important to share how these experiences shaped my vision of library work and, in the case of CPL, to reflect back on my first experience working in a library. I think it's also within these experiences that I got to utilize the leadership and communication skills that goals four and five are centered around. I faced situations that required me to think about ethics, management, informed decision making, and what kind of leader I wanted to be.

The remaining four artifacts are the proposals, the mis-en-page project, and the presentation. The proposals are very different from each other in that one is a hypothetical programming proposal I wrote for my LIS 707 course, and the other is a proposal for a book conservation project for my LIS 804 Introduction to Book Binding course. Both proposals were chosen because they reflected goals three and four. Goals three and four come up the most in these proposals, as they are also reflected in the mis-en-page project and the presentation. The presentation also relates to goal five, as it relates to effectively communicating concepts to a group of people.

Overall, I wanted my e-portfolio to reflect my interests in archives, applied theory, teaching, and designing platforms to share information. It was through courses like the Introduction to Archival Practice, Facilitating User Knowledge, and Early Books and Manuscripts that I was able to broaden my interests and explore the possibilities of what a career in the library and information field could look like. Having the experience of going on site visits to libraries and archives in LIS 711 helped to cement my desire to work as an archivist and with special collections material, but it was in these other courses that I realized I wanted to take on other identities and roles within the field.

When I arrived at Dominican I was mainly concerned with becoming an archivist exclusively, but as I've taken this moment to reflect I think about how important it is to me to be active in making material available to the public — not just stewarding material but creating opportunity for its use. Currently I work as a digital asset management assistant at the Newberry Library. I process our digitized and born digital material; uploading it to the DAMS, creating and cleaning metadata, publicizing it, and archiving it are all parts of my daily tasks. I've become very comfortable working on the purely digital side of librarianship and I still have a lot to learn.

For me, working with digital assets has led to seeing more of the collection and I appreciate this time of familiarizing myself with a broader scope of material. I do think, however, that in the future I would like my career in libraries and archives to turn back to the physical. For me the ideal career track is one where I end up working with artist's books and archives or as a project archivist. Alongside this, I would like to take on teaching and programming within a library or creating interactive web content that promotes collection use. The ethos I've developed while at Dominican has led me to seek a balance between preservation and public use.
