Artifact 1

Affective Archiving

How can affect theory help archivists create new frameworks to work with?

PDF  ·  LIS 755 ↓ Download PDF
MLIS Student Learning Goals and Outcomes

This essay reflects Goals 1, 2, and 4 by engaging with affect theory as a framework for rethinking archival practice in relation to community, trauma, and professional identity. Drawing on scholarly literature in archival studies and critical theory, it demonstrates how theoretical perspectives from outside library science can be applied to real professional situations — particularly in archives stewarding materials from historically marginalized communities. The paper reflects Outcomes 1b and 4a by using a specific theoretical model to evaluate and guide practice, and Outcome 2b by examining how institutional approaches to information access can either support or harm the communities they serve.

Artifact 2

Placemaking and the Library

How can we borrow the concept of placemaking from urban studies and incorporate it into the library?

PDF  ·  LIS 707 ↓ Download PDF
MLIS Student Learning Goals and Outcomes

This essay reflects Goals 1, 2, and 4 by borrowing the concept of placemaking from urban studies and applying it to the management and programming of public libraries. It demonstrates Outcomes 1b and 4a through the application of an interdisciplinary theoretical framework to library practice, showing how understanding how communities produce and inhabit space can inform how library workers approach their roles. The paper also reflects Outcome 2c by developing a response to assessed community information and social needs, and Outcome 4c by examining the teaching and learning dimensions of library space and programming.

Artifact 3

Neutrality & The Library

Should libraries be political?

PDF  ·  LIS 701 ↓ Download PDF
MLIS Student Learning Goals and Outcomes

This essay reflects Goals 1 and 2 by interrogating the concept of neutrality as it is constructed and practiced within librarianship, arguing that the library's commitment to inclusivity is itself a political stance. It engages Outcomes 1a and 1b by tracing how core professional values have evolved and how they shape practice in diverse settings, drawing on scholarly debate about the limits of institutional apoliticism. The paper also reflects Outcome 2b by examining how the framing of library neutrality has real consequences for intellectual freedom and equitable access to information.

Artifact 4

Net Neutrality & Equity of Access

How does net neutrality relate to information equity, and what is the role of the information professional in navigating its impact on patrons?

PDF  ·  LIS 701 ↓ Download PDF
MLIS Student Learning Goals and Outcomes

This essay reflects Goals 2, 4, and 5 by providing a comprehensive overview of net neutrality policy and its direct implications for information equity and the library profession. It engages Outcomes 2b and 2c by analyzing how shifts in internet regulation affect public access to information and the ability of libraries to serve communities that depend on open connectivity. The paper also reflects Outcomes 4a and 5a by connecting policy history to professional ethics and making the case for why information professionals must advocate for net neutrality as a core equity issue.
